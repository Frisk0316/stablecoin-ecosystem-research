## Supported Themes

- Singleness, elasticity, and integrity remain the core central-bank framing
  from BIS and related public-sector sources.
- The Federal Reserve source set now supports bank-intermediation and
  financial-stability analysis. A 2025 FEDS Note links stablecoin adoption to
  safer/liquid reserves and highlights vulnerabilities from intermediation
  chains, vertical integration, and retail wallet adoption (`CLAIM_044`,
  `CLAIM_045`).
- Federal Reserve banking-system work supports a conditional deposit-impact
  frame: bank deposits may shrink, recycle, or restructure depending on
  stablecoin demand, what assets users convert, and how issuers allocate
  reserves (`CLAIM_046`). Credit effects should be routed through deposit
  volume, deposit composition, funding costs, and liquidity (`CLAIM_047`).
- Federal Reserve IFDP evidence distinguishes a two-tier stablecoin structure
  from a narrow-bank model: the former can support issuance and credit creation,
  while the latter may strengthen peg credibility at the cost of more direct
  bank disintermediation (`CLAIM_048`).
- IMF evidence supports the run-risk/reserve-design trade-off: safer backing
  lowers run risk but can reduce issuer profitability and private incentives
  to choose conservative reserves (`CLAIM_049`).
- IMF payment-system evidence supports the competitive-threat framing for
  incumbent payment firms, with an event-study estimate of an 18% value decline
  after stablecoin-supportive policy news (`CLAIM_050`).

## Guardrails

- Do not state that stablecoins mechanically reduce bank lending. The supported
  position is conditional and depends on user conversion assets, reserve
  allocation, and whether deposits are recycled through banking channels.
- Separate retail payment adoption, DeFi/liquidity use, issuer reserve demand,
  and bank-funding effects.
- Treat CBDC and tokenized deposits as alternative public/private design
  responses, not as automatic substitutes for every stablecoin use case.
