# Executive Summary

Version: v0.3 interim
Last updated: 2026-05-15

This document summarises the stablecoin ecosystem research project that
underpins the 2026-06-27 reading-group session. The summary is intended for
participants who want a one-page orientation before reading the chapter
drafts and the claim table; each finding is anchored to one or more
`CLAIM_XXX` entries in `03_claim_tables/claim_table_master.csv`, which in
turn cites the primary source registered in `01_sources/source_registry.csv`.

## Scope

The project treats stablecoins as a structural question rather than a market
question. The unit of analysis is **the system of issuers, reserves,
custodians, derivatives counterparties, payment-system participants, and
legal regimes through which a stablecoin liability is created, held,
transferred, and ultimately redeemed (or wound down)**. The project
distinguishes three product categories that should not be aggregated into
a single risk class:

1. **Fiat-backed payment stablecoins** issued by a corporate or trust entity
   against bank deposits, short-dated Treasuries, repurchase agreements,
   government money-market fund shares, and similar high-quality liquid
   assets (USDC, USDT, PYUSD, USDP, USDG, RLUSD, FDUSD, GUSD).
2. **Crypto / RWA-collateralised protocol stablecoins** generated by
   user-deposited collateral into governance-controlled smart-contract
   vaults, with peg stability maintained through over-collateralisation,
   liquidation auctions, and governance backstops (DAI / USDS).
3. **Synthetic-dollar stablecoins** maintained at 1:1 collateralisation by
   delta-neutral hedging of backing assets via short perpetual futures
   positions on derivative exchanges, with off-exchange custody of the
   spot leg (USDe).

A fourth proposed entity, USDPT, is treated separately throughout. Its
issuer (Anchorage Digital Bank), launch announcement, and infrastructure
partnerships are documented, but its product terms, reserve report,
contract addresses, and end-to-end settlement workflow remain absent from
the public record at the v0.3 cut-off.

## Headline findings

1. **Fiat-backed stablecoins are not external to the dollar system; they
   are a tokenised wrapper around regulated dollar money-market and
   banking-system instruments.** USDC reserves comprise bank deposits,
   overnight reverse Treasury repo, sub-three-month Treasuries, and the
   Circle Reserve Fund (`CLAIM_026`, `CLAIM_027`). USDT's Q1 2026
   reserve report and May 2026 issuer release support approximately
   US\$141bn of direct and indirect Treasury-bill exposure plus
   non-cash exposures (~US\$20bn physical gold and ~US\$7bn Bitcoin)
   that prevent USDT from being modelled as a pure cash equivalent
   (`CLAIM_028`, `CLAIM_029`). Paxos USD Stablecoins (USDP, PYUSD,
   USDG) are restricted by contract to FDIC-insured deposits, direct
   or repurchase investments in U.S. government-guaranteed debt
   instruments, and money-market funds composed of such instruments
   (`CLAIM_075`). Gemini Dollar reserves are held across three
   account categories: FDIC-insured omnibus accounts, money-market
   accounts holding government-securities MMFs, and Treasury accounts
   (`CLAIM_078`).

2. **Direct holder redemption rights are typically gated by
   counterparty contracts and are narrower than product-page
   "redeemable at par" language suggests.** USDC direct redemption
   requires a Circle Mint account in good standing (`CLAIM_058`);
   USDT requires verified-customer status with explicit prohibited
   persons and jurisdictions (`CLAIM_060`, `CLAIM_061`); FDUSD
   requires an FD121 Account that excludes U.S. individuals
   (`CLAIM_062`, `CLAIM_064`); the unified Paxos USD Stablecoin
   Agreement limits direct purchase and redemption of PYUSD, USDP,
   and USDG to Paxos Customers, with Non-Customer Token Holders
   defined but excluded from direct rights (`CLAIM_073`); Gemini's
   user agreement restricts GUSD creation and redemption to Gemini
   Customers and explicitly disclaims any relationship with
   non-customers who obtain or use GUSD (`CLAIM_077`). USDe restricts
   direct issuance and redemption to whitelisted Mint Users that
   complete KYC/AML/onboarding, excluding U.S. users
   (`CLAIM_065`, `CLAIM_066`).

3. **Reserve attestations are not financial-statement audits.** This
   distinction is preserved throughout the research and is now
   reflected in the federal statute. USDC, USDT, RLUSD, FDUSD, GUSD,
   USDP, and USDe all rely on examination or assurance reports issued
   under AICPA or ISAE 3000 / 3000R attestation standards rather than
   on full audits. The GENIUS Act now requires permitted payment
   stablecoin issuers to publish monthly reserve composition with
   examination by a registered public accounting firm, with CEO/CFO
   certification subject to criminal-penalty exposure for knowingly
   false certifications (`CLAIM_085`); MiCA imposes a parallel
   monthly disclosure regime, with significant EMT issuers facing a
   six-month independent audit cadence (`CLAIM_097`).

4. **Four jurisdictions now provide overlapping but distinct statutory
   baselines for payment stablecoins.** The U.S. GENIUS Act (Public
   Law 119-27) restricts issuance to "permitted payment stablecoin
   issuers" (`CLAIM_082`), imposes an exhaustively enumerated reserve
   composition list with a 93-day Treasury maturity cap and overnight
   repo terms (`CLAIM_084`), prohibits payment of any form of
   interest or yield to holders solely in connection with holding,
   use, or retention of the stablecoin (`CLAIM_086`), and creates a
   customer-priority rule in issuer insolvency proceedings
   (`CLAIM_087`). MiCA distinguishes asset-referenced tokens (ARTs,
   `CLAIM_069` to `CLAIM_071`, `CLAIM_094` to `CLAIM_096`) from
   e-money tokens (EMTs, `CLAIM_042`, `CLAIM_043`, `CLAIM_097`),
   imposes a permanent holder redemption right against the issuer for
   ARTs and at-any-time par-value redemption for EMTs, prohibits
   interest on ARTs at the statutory level, and requires recovery and
   redemption (wind-down) plans with temporary-administrator
   designation. The Bank of England's 2025 consultation on
   sterling-denominated systemic stablecoins requires at least 40%
   unremunerated central bank deposits plus up to 60% short-term UK
   government debt, with a step-up regime allowing up to 95% UK
   gilts at launch (`CLAIM_090`), and introduces quantitative holding
   limits (per-coin retail £20,000 and business £10 million)
   without analogue in either GENIUS or MiCA (`CLAIM_091`). NYDFS
   guidance remains the operative regime for GUSD and RLUSD,
   imposing a lawful-holder T+2 redemption default and monthly CPA
   examination (`CLAIM_036` to `CLAIM_040`).

5. **Central-bank and IMF analysis frames stablecoin growth as a
   conditional transmission channel rather than a deterministic
   threat to banks.** Federal Reserve IFDP 1334 argues that the
   impact on banking and credit provision depends on inflow sources
   and reserve composition, with a two-tiered banking model
   supporting both stablecoin issuance and traditional credit
   creation, while a narrow-bank model increases disintermediation
   but strengthens the fiat peg (`CLAIM_048`). IMF working papers
   model unregulated private stablecoin issuance as suboptimal
   because issuers may hold risky assets to maximise profits and so
   raise run risk, with safe-asset backing improving stability but
   reducing issuer profitability without alternative revenue
   mechanisms (`CLAIM_049`), and find via event study that
   U.S. legislation supporting stablecoin payments reduced the
   market value of listed incumbent payment firms by 18%, or
   approximately US\$300bn, consistent with markets expecting
   stablecoins to increase payment-sector competition
   (`CLAIM_050`). The research treats these as market-expectation
   evidence rather than realised payment-adoption evidence.

6. **Crypto / RWA-collateralised and synthetic-dollar stablecoins
   participate in dollar money markets through different channels and
   should not be aggregated with fiat-backed issuers when estimating
   stablecoin-driven Treasury demand.** The MakerDAO Multi-Collateral
   Dai (MCD) Protocol generates Dai / USDS through overcollateralisation
   by governance-approved collateral, with Maker Vault liquidation
   handled by automated Collateral Auction / Reverse Collateral Auction
   mechanics with Protocol Surplus or MKR (now SKY) dilution as the
   backstop (`CLAIM_098`, `CLAIM_099`), and pays holders a Sky Savings
   Rate via sUSDS (`CLAIM_100`, `CLAIM_102`). USDe maintains its
   1:1 peg through delta-neutral hedging via short perpetual futures
   positions on centralised derivatives exchanges, with backing
   assets held in Off-Exchange Settlement custody and never
   transferred to exchange counterparties (`CLAIM_103`, `CLAIM_104`),
   monthly custodian attestations stating that no backing assets
   reside directly on exchange partners (`CLAIM_105`), and a Reserve
   Fund of stablecoins plus a smaller ETH allocation controlled by a
   4/10 multi-sig (`CLAIM_106`).

## Limitations and unresolved questions

The research is **not** a complete report. Notable gaps are catalogued in
`00_project_management/unresolved_open_questions.md` and include:

- **USDPT / Western Union digital-asset network.** Launch
  announcements, infrastructure partnerships (Fireblocks), and
  Anchorage Digital Bank's GENIUS Act comment letter are archived,
  but product terms, reserve report, contract addresses, and an
  end-to-end customer-to-customer settlement workflow are not in the
  archive. The chapter on USDPT is therefore deliberately
  conditional. The research must not claim that USDPT replaces SWIFT,
  correspondent banking, Fedwire, CHIPS, or Western Union's
  consumer-facing remittance rails without workflow documentation.
- **Adjusted on-chain payment volume.** Visa Onchain Analytics
  adjusted-volume export, Artemis methodology beyond `CLAIM_054`,
  and McKinsey / Artemis joint analysis (`MCKINSEY_ARTEMIS_001`)
  remain outside the archive. Raw on-chain transfer volume must not
  be equated with realised payment demand.
- **Sub-statutory U.S. material.** The CLARITY Act's stablecoin-
  relevant sections have not yet been isolated from its broader
  market-structure provisions.
- **Cross-border equivalence.** The research has not yet evaluated
  whether the BoE 2025 backing-asset regime would qualify as
  "substantially similar" to the GENIUS Act for foreign-issuer
  reciprocity purposes, nor how the MiCA Article 56(4) derogation
  for non-euro Member States interacts with significant-EMT
  supervisory transfer to EBA.
- **Taiwan-specific synthesis.** Central-bank claims at
  `CLAIM_044` to `CLAIM_050` are general; Taiwan-specific
  implications around NTD stablecoin proposals, FX monitoring,
  digital dollarisation, deposit substitution, and monetary
  sovereignty have not yet been drawn from existing CBC and TWFRC
  sources.

## Methodological commitments

Throughout the project, every substantive claim that appears in the
chapter drafts or in this summary is traceable to a row in the claim
table, with `source_id`, `page_or_section`, evidence quotation, and
confidence level. Several guardrails apply:

- Attestation reports are not described as audits unless the source
  itself uses that term for the relevant engagement.
- SWIFT is treated as a messaging layer, not a funds-settlement
  system.
- Raw on-chain transfer volume is never equated with realised
  payment demand.
- Fiat-backed payment stablecoins, crypto / RWA-collateralised
  protocol stablecoins, and synthetic-dollar instruments are kept
  in separate categories.
- MiCA ART and MiCA EMT rules are kept separate; significant-token
  obligations are kept separate from the general ART / EMT
  baselines.
- Where evidence is missing, the open question is preserved rather
  than guessed.

The claim table contains 106 entries at v0.3 close (`CLAIM_001`
through `CLAIM_106`). The source registry contains 130 rows.
Validation scripts `08_scripts/validate_claim_table.py` and
`08_scripts/check_missing_sources.py` both pass at the v0.3 cut-off.

## How to read this package

1. Begin with `05_chapter_drafts/chapter_01_stablecoins_as_onchain_dollar_system.md`
   for the framing and `05_chapter_drafts/chapter_09_conclusion.md` for the
   synthesis.
2. Move to `05_chapter_drafts/chapter_02_issuer_comparison.md` for
   issuer-by-issuer evidence and to `05_chapter_drafts/chapter_04_law_and_regulation.md`
   for the four-jurisdiction comparison.
3. Use `03_claim_tables/claim_table_master.csv` as the traceability
   layer for any specific assertion; use `01_sources/source_registry.csv`
   to reach the original document.
4. Use the chapter-4 cross-jurisdiction comparison table as the
   one-screen reference for regulatory differences.
